We screen hundreds of applications a week for engineering & data science roles at Axle Energy. Most give no hint that the applicant has engaged with what we do at all.

That’s understandable - applying for jobs is time consuming, and there’s an arms race between algorithmic applications and algorithmic CV review (which we don’t use). But if you’ve read this far, you might be in the minority with the time & energy to submit a *really good* application. If so, read on.

Your aim is to make it easy to spot that you understand what startups look for in engineers. The question you need to answer, with a resounding yes, is: **will this person help us succeed**?

Your CV is not a summary of your life: it’s a tool. It should solve the hiring manager’s problem in a way that serves your interest - getting you an interview. Think of it as the Amazon buy-now button, effortlessly ushering me towards the action you want me to take.

Mostly this means *removing* things - which conveniently keeps your CV to the 1 page you need.

<aside>

This should generalise to other &lt;100 person startups, and poorly beyond that. It holds when the screener’s incentives are aligned with the startup’s success. If they’re optimising for something else (a KPI, a promotion, a keyword-matching algorithm), your mileage may vary.

</aside>

# What startups care about

## A track record of helping companies achieve their goals

Startups succeed by identifying real-world problems, solving them, and getting paid. They want to hire people who’ve solved valuable problems before.

Refract all of your experience through this lens. Describe:

- what the company was doing
- what problem they needed your help with
- how you got it done

Numbers (£, users, transactions) are great, but optional. Just explaining a few problems and how you solved them will put you in the top 10% of candidates.

Matching the company’s exact tools is a bonus, not a requirement: someone who solves real problems in Javascript can adapt to Python, Go, or Rust.

## Drive

Startups are tiring and need a constant injection of energy. We want to hire ambitious, driven people who bring energy to those around them. Such people often do unusual things with their free time. Maybe they don’t have much any more - they’re parents, or running a company, or caring for a relative - but there’s usually a hint that when they *did*, they used it.

The best thing on your CV is an interesting side project someone can interact with. Top marks if it solves a *real* problem (please dudes, no more weight-lifting apps 🤙), you got it in front of a stranger, and you tried to monetise it.

There are lots of other ways to show drive. I find signal in:

- Making things - writing, art, dance, music - and getting them out into the world, especially if it took courage
- Playing sport at a high level. This demands dedication and discipline - the less talented you are the better.
- Taking your hobbies a bit too far. Several of our engineers have built extensions to games they love. I don’t game, but I have a lot of time for anyone in the most intense 1% of whatever they do.

Don’t confuse “participating” with “demonstrating grit, excellence, or courage”. Being in a play isn’t impressive, but writing one is; playing 5-a-side isn’t, playing for England is; listening to the radio isn’t, building radios is. You get the idea.

A note on bias: men are more likely to be *explicitly* ambitious in hiring - lots of blokes think they’ll end up as CEOs/Founders/Philosopher-Kings. If you’re less comfortable displaying naked ambition in interviews, it’s even more important to show-not-tell in your CV.

## Social proof of excellence

If you’ve been promoted, say so. It’s reassuring that people who’ve worked with you rate you enough to give you more responsibility.

In rigid promotion regimes (Google, McKinsey etc.) candidates sometimes note the average tenure at each level and that they were promoted ahead of schedule. Helpful, if slightly obnoxious - a worthwhile trade-off.

## A nice-looking CV

Spoiler: startup founders aren’t just picky and demanding, we’re capricious and vain.

The joy of being a startup engineer is that the gap between your work and the outside world is paper thin - no layers of bureaucracy & QA to ship through. But that also means there’s no designer or PM to make your work fit for human consumption. So it’s reassuring when someone produces a CV that looks good and has no typos. After all, I’m hiring you to *build things* - and if you can’t produce a single sheet of error-free, decent-looking A4, it raises immediate concerns about shipping complex software directly into the arms of clients.

The best engineers I’ve worked with are also excellent writers, which makes them effective collaborators in async or highly scaled environments. The premium on this has gone *up* since Claude started vomiting out design docs.

Never submit a Word Doc, unless you enjoy seeing your achievements regurgitated with random alignment and missing fonts.

## That you will be enjoyable to work with

<aside>

I almost cut this section because it’s guaranteed to get me in trouble.

Companies should be careful about selecting people “who will fit in well”. Done lazily, it’s a fast road to nepotism and bias.

But cultural alignment matters for small groups doing hard things. Everybody who joins a startup shapes the culture. If you want a fun place to work where people form bonds strong enough to overcome adversity with good humour, that should influence your hiring.

</aside>

Unlike at a BigCorp, the person reading your CV will likely work closely with you for many hours a week.

So drop a few hints that this will be tolerable, even enjoyable. I’m a sucker for a sense of humour in a CV. I’m told that’s not a fair thing to screen for, but it’s probably true for everyone screening CVs: if you can make them smile, you have a better shot.

This doesn’t mean you need to be a social wizard. Most people at startups are a bit intense, fairly nerdy, and more than a little addicted to what they do. We’re looking for *some* personality, not a *specific* one.

# What startups do not care about

Keeping this punchy, since it’s a bit mean:

- Professional certifications of any kind. Seriously, bin them - especially waffly ones like “Agile Principles Qualified” or “AWS trusts me to sell AWS”. These are red rags to angry founder bulls who started companies to escape corporate claptrap.
- How many technologies you know. Figure out which ones your target company cares about, mention those, and tie them to a problem you solved.
- Your ability to use MS Office. Your ability to read, write, and feed yourself is also taken as read.
- Papers you’ve authored, unless you’re applying somewhere deep-techy that needs your specific expertise. Sorry - this was a bitter pill for me too.
- Whether you have a driving licence.
- Irrelevant roles e.g. bartender, dogsitter, lifeguard.

# The cover letter

The cover letter is your second shot on goal, and its job is to customise your application to the company.

This is online dating: your CV is your Hinge profile (a highly selective account of your most desirable attributes), and your cover letter is the first DM. Founders are vain - make them feel special.

Here’s one, which speaks to our company’s mission:

> I want to work on climate change, the defining challenge of our generation. I’ve done a lot of research, and Axle Energy looks like the place I can have the biggest impact on climate change in the next decade. Decarbonizing the power sector is instrumental to cleaning up the rest of the economy, and if I can play a meaningful role in that then I’ll consider my career a success. I think that together we can decarbonize the grid - looking forward to discussing.
> 

Here’s another, which is more focused on personal growth and drive:

> The amount you guys have achieved in the first 2 years is insane. I prioritize working with people I can learn from, and you’re clearly a crazy talented bunch. I’m extremely motivated to work hard and make a dent in the universe, and I think Axle is a place where I can find like-minded people to grow with, ship insane amounts of software, and build something great.
> 

Or maybe you wanna keep it brief:

> Axle is winning. I like winning. You should hire me, and we’ll win bigger - which will be great for the grid and the climate.
> 

These are all fake, and I’d interview all of them. The signal from a thoughtful, eccentric cover letter is so strong I almost wouldn’t bother with the CV.

# In summary

1. Show you understand what drives a company’s success, and that you deliver it. Remove anything that implies otherwise.
2. Emphasise the qualities startups value: determination, creativity, and ambition.
3. Have a little fun. Show some personality and make someone smile.
